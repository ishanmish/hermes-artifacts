Iquitos ayahuasca retreat briefing

Note: the city is Iquitos, Peru (often misspelled “Equitos”). It is one of the main international hubs for ayahuasca tourism because it is a large Amazon city with an airport, retreat infrastructure, and access to river/jungle settings.

1) Who usually travels to Iquitos for this?

There does not appear to be a clean public dataset saying “ayahuasca tourists to Iquitos come from X countries.” The best-supported picture is:

  • Normal Peru tourism is dominated by nearby Latin American countries plus the US/Europe.
  • Ayahuasca tourism specifically seems to skew more toward North America, Western Europe, Australia/New Zealand, and Israel, based on academic/guidebook/review-platform signals.
  • Nearly everyone funnels through Lima because Iquitos is not connected by road to the rest of Peru and public flight data shows Iquitos/IQT mainly as a domestic destination.
  • Common route: international flight -> Lima -> Iquitos -> taxi/mototaxi/center pickup -> jungle/road/river retreat.
  • If coming from Colombia, another adventure route is Bogota/Medellin -> Leticia -> cross to Santa Rosa, Peru -> fast boat to Iquitos, but that is more time-consuming and less predictable than flying via Lima.

Practical implication: you will meet a mixed crowd — backpackers, spiritual/psychonaut travelers, trauma-healing seekers, older wellness travelers, and some people doing it as part of a longer Peru/Colombia/Amazon trip. It is not just hippies, but the atmosphere around retreats is usually alternative/spiritual rather than luxury-resort mainstream.

2) Where around Iquitos do retreats happen?

The main retreat geography:

  1. Iquitos city / airport staging
  • Most people spend at least 1 night in Iquitos before and after.
  • Useful for supplies, SIM, cash, food, pharmacy, and flight/boat buffers.
  1. Iquitos-Nauta road / Maralillos-type areas
  • Many lower-cost and mid-range centers are off this road.
  • Easier transport, cheaper logistics, less remote.
  1. Nanay / Llanchama / nearby jungle communities
  • Forest-ish atmosphere without being extremely remote.
  • Includes some well-known centers and smaller operators.
  1. Allpahuayo-Mishana / Riosbo / Mishana side
  • More Amazon nature reserve/community feel.
  • Often used by structured retreat centers.
  1. Tamshiyacu / river-access areas
  • More removed from Iquitos, deeper jungle feel.
  • Big-name centers exist here, often pricier.
  1. Nauta / Ucayali / remote river settings
  • More immersive, more travel time, potentially more expensive/logistically complex.
  1. Village-clinic / shaman-family settings
  • Usually cheaper and more traditional, but harder to verify online.
  • Quality varies dramatically; you need stronger due diligence.

3) Places/centers people commonly mention

Higher visibility / established / often not cheap:

  • Temple of the Way of Light: famous, structured, strong reputation, usually expensive.
  • Arkana Amazon: polished, international, higher-end.
  • Ayahuasca Foundation / Riosbo: established, structured, mid-to-high.
  • Blue Morpho: long-standing name, usually not budget.

Lower-cost or more modest leads worth researching carefully:

  1. Kapitari / Estacion Kapitari
  • Often comes up as a more affordable, older-school option near Iquitos.
  • Better fit if you want simple, traditional, less luxury-coded.
  • Still verify current facilitator, medical screening, group size, and exact inclusions.
  1. Marosa Healing Center
  • Smaller/family-style Shipibo-oriented retreat positioning.
  • Often lower than luxury centers, but not necessarily cheap.
  • Ask about translator/facilitator support if you do not speak Spanish.
  1. Paojilhuasca
  • Mentioned on retreat platforms as a lower-cost Iquitos-area option.
  • Needs careful verification because platform listings can be stale.
  1. Selva Madre
  • More established than a pure word-of-mouth shaman, but not as luxury as the biggest names.
  • Check current pricing and independent reviews.
  1. Dreamglade
  • Well-known, more comfortable/polished than the cheapest options.
  • Often a good middle-ground but may not be the cheapest.

Important: truly “not companies but shamans” are often found locally through word-of-mouth, hostels, other travelers, or local contacts, not polished websites. That can reduce cost, but increases risk: no screening, poor emergency planning, unclear consent norms, no translator, and no accountability. For ayahuasca, cheap can become stupid-cheap very quickly.

4) How to find a lower-cost shaman-led retreat without getting reckless

Must-have questions:

  • Who is the maestro/maestra? What lineage/community? How many years serving medicine?
  • Is there a sober facilitator present all night?
  • Is there medical/psychological screening before acceptance?
  • What medications are disallowed? Specifically ask about SSRIs/SNRIs/MAOIs, stimulants, lithium, antipsychotics, blood-pressure meds, heart issues, bipolar/psychosis history.
  • How many guests per ceremony?
  • How many ceremonies in the package?
  • Is there integration support after ceremonies?
  • What is the emergency plan and how far is the nearest clinic/hospital?
  • Are ceremonies gender-safe? Any rules around touch, private sessions, nudity, isolation, or “healing massages”?
  • Are tobacco/mapacho, kambo, bufo, or other medicines included or upsold?
  • What exactly is included: airport pickup, Iquitos hotel, meals, dieta, translation, lodging, laundry, post-retreat transport?

Green flags:

  • They say no to some people after screening.
  • They ask about psychiatric history and medications.
  • They have clear ceremony rules and consent boundaries.
  • They do not promise miracle cures.
  • They have recent independent reviews across more than one platform.
  • They can explain emergency logistics clearly.

Red flags:

  • “Everyone can drink, no problem.”
  • No medication screening.
  • Very high ceremony density with little rest.
  • Sexualized “healing,” private isolation with a shaman, or pressure to surrender boundaries.
  • Hard upsells to expensive master-plant dietas you did not request.
  • No sober facilitator/translator.
  • Only Instagram/WhatsApp presence with no independent reviews.
  • Refusal to disclose group size or exact price.

5) How many days do people actually stay?

Realistic formats:

  • 3 days / 2 nights: too short for most first-timers; usually one ceremony; not much time to settle or integrate.
  • 5 days: minimum-ish if you are time-constrained; usually 2 ceremonies.
  • 7 days / 6 nights: most sensible first-timer retreat length; often 3 ceremonies.
  • 10 days: stronger if you want depth without making it your whole trip; often 4 ceremonies.
  • 12-14 days: common for serious retreats; more expensive and more intense.
  • 18+ days: for committed dieta/deeper work, not a casual first experience.

Best first-timer plan: 9-11 calendar days total.

  • 1-2 nights in Iquitos before the retreat.
  • 7-day / 6-night retreat.
  • 1 night in Iquitos after before flying/boating onward.

Do not fly out immediately after the final ceremony if you can avoid it. Give yourself a buffer.

6) Budget reality

Very rough all-in ranges from Lima, excluding international flight to Peru:

  • Budget 5-7 day retreat: US$1,200-2,200 all-in if you keep hotels/food simple and find a modest center.
  • Sensible 7-day first-timer setup: US$1,800-3,100 all-in.
  • More established 10-day retreat: US$3,300-4,800 all-in.
  • Comfortable/high-end 12-14 day retreat: US$3,300-6,500+ all-in.

Extra costs people forget:

  • Lima -> Iquitos flights.
  • Iquitos buffer hotel nights.
  • Airport/retreat transport.
  • Travel insurance.
  • Cash tips/donations.
  • Laundry/supplies.
  • Post-retreat decompression days.
  • If coming from Leticia by boat: extra border/transport/buffer costs.

For your preference (“not too costly”), I would not start with the luxury centers. Shortlist Kapitari, Marosa, Paojilhuasca, Selva Madre, and maybe Dreamglade, then contact them directly with the due-diligence questions above.

7) What is the atmosphere in Iquitos?

Iquitos is intense, humid, noisy, river-city Amazon energy — mototaxis, markets, riverfront, colonial-era buildings, jungle-tour operators, expats, backpackers, spiritual seekers, and a lot of informal hustle. It is not a peaceful spa town. The peaceful part is usually outside the city at the retreat.

Expect:

  • Hot, wet, mosquito-heavy climate.
  • Chaotic traffic and mototaxis.
  • Friendly but salesy tourism scene.
  • Lots of ayahuasca advertising and word-of-mouth recommendations.
  • A mix of real indigenous/vegetalista traditions and heavily commercialized spiritual tourism.
  • Good staging city, not necessarily somewhere you want to spend weeks unless you like frontier river towns.

8) Other things to do around Iquitos

Good add-ons:

  • Belén Market: chaotic, interesting, but go carefully; watch belongings and avoid dubious wildlife products.
  • Amazon river / Nanay river boat trips.
  • Pilpintuwasi butterfly farm / animal rescue-style visits.
  • Manatee rescue center.
  • Quistococha lake/park.
  • Allpahuayo-Mishana reserve hikes/birding.
  • Pacaya-Samiria National Reserve multi-day jungle trips — better if you have extra time and budget.
  • Riverfront/malecón in Iquitos.
  • Simple food/juice/market exploration.

Things I would avoid around a retreat:

  • Heavy partying before/after ceremonies.
  • Alcohol/drugs close to ceremonies.
  • Trying to stack kambo/bufo/San Pedro/ayahuasca because someone sells you a “combo.”
  • Tight onward travel the morning after a ceremony.

9) My recommendation for you

If you want a real experience but do not want to overspend:

  1. Plan 9-11 days total around Iquitos.
  2. Do a 7-day retreat, not a 3-day one.
  3. Choose a modest but reviewable center rather than a random WhatsApp-only shaman.
  4. Start with Kapitari, Marosa, Paojilhuasca, Selva Madre, and Dreamglade.
  5. Email/WhatsApp all five with the same screening questions and compare total price, ceremony count, group size, facilitator/translator presence, medical screening, emergency plan, current maestro/maestra, and recent dates/reviews.
  6. Spend 1-2 nights in Iquitos before, 1 night after.
  7. If you still want the more adventurous Colombia route, do it for the journey, not because it is easier than flying.

10) Safety / medical caution

Ayahuasca can be psychologically and medically risky. It is especially risky with some psychiatric histories, heart/blood-pressure issues, and medications including serotonergic antidepressants, MAOIs, lithium, antipsychotics, stimulants, and other substances. Do not treat a retreat center’s casual reassurance as medical clearance. If any of this applies, talk to a qualified clinician before considering it.
